Don't Trust the Label —
Measure Reality Every Cycle

What I learned from teaching an algorithm to share fish fairly.

An experiment in fairness, bias, and the hidden cost of trusting reputation over reality.

Slide 1 · The Key Lesson

Don't trust the label — measure reality every cycle.

  • I built a rule that sounded perfectly fair: always help whoever needs it most. But it created the worst unfairness of all — I call this the Capacity Trap.
  • The reason: it ranked fishing zones by their old reputation ("the best zone"), not by how many fish were actually there right now. So it kept sending the neediest people to zones that were already fished empty.
  • That one surprise shaped my whole design: rank by what's really there this moment, recalculate every cycle, and never trust yesterday's numbers.
Slide 2 · My Stance on Fairness

Rawlsian, stock-aware, group-blind — and honest about its price.

What it stands for

Protect the worst-off first (lift the floor). Judge on current reality, not reputation. Never look at which group someone is in — only their health and the live fish stock.

What it gives up

It won't squeeze out the maximum total catch — I refuse to hand the richest zones to the most skilled group. And in a true famine, it can't save everyone.

"A perfectly fair algorithm is mathematically impossible on top of an unequal starting line."

Slide 3 · How I Worked With the AI

Hypothesis-driven — and adversarial when it mattered.

  • My loop every time: predict → run a big batch (50 times) → observe → explain → connect. I never trusted one noisy run.
  • I used the AI to teach me concepts (fairness math, hidden bias), read the metrics, fix bugs, and build a test harness that tried ~11 different rules against the real engine.
  • But I checked its claims. I caught it trusting a score that only counted survivors (a third had already died), and a "need-based = 100%" result that couldn't be repeated.
  • The rule I learned: verify the baseline before you optimize against it.
Slide 4 · What I've Found So Far

Four experiments, four surprises.

Experiment 1

No rule wins everything

Every rule is best at something and worst at something else. The surprise: lifting the floor also raised the total — helping the weakest didn't cost the group.

Experiment 2

Hiding the group changed nothing

I removed the group label — bias still rode in through wealth as a stand-in. And two fairness goals could never both be met (0 / 50 runs).

Experiment 3

Advantage compounds

The poorest group froze at a tiny catch. The Capacity Trap flipped outcomes upside down. And in famine, survival caps at about 74%.

My Constitution · in progress

Stock-aware proportional fill

100% in normal times, ~74% in famine (ties the best rule), fixes the trap, and has the lowest unfairness of all ~11 variants.

Slide 5 · Real-World Connections

The AI industry is playing the exact same game.

  • The scarce fish = compute, data, and talent. Group A = the giant frontier labs. Group C = open-source projects and startups.
  • Benchmarks are the noisy observation, and compute spending is the wealth stand-in — "10,000 H100 chips is unmistakably a big lab, no matter what name is on the door."
  • The GPU shortage is the famine. And using 2022 safety rules to judge 2025 models is exactly the stale-data mistake from the Capacity Trap.
  • "Group A writes the ruler that measures everyone" — the incumbents set the benchmarks that decide who gets access.
Slide 6 · Takeaways

What surprised me and changed my thinking.

  • Helping the worst-off raised the average. Fairness and efficiency don't always fight each other.
  • The "fair" rule caused the worst harm (the Capacity Trap) — and the group it ignored actually thrived.
  • Hiding the group label did nothing — bias simply rode in on a proxy.
  • 100% is impossible by sharing alone — it's just arithmetic: 9 fish can't feed 18 hungry people. Only redistribution fixes a famine, and an allocation rule can't redistribute.
  • The big shift: "The algorithm isn't the problem — it's a mirror. Fairness is a choice about whose harm counts, not a calculation."
